## 💨 Why Wind Matters

Wind energy harnesses the power of the wind to generate electricity, making use of wind turbines to capture the wind's kinetic energy and convert it into usable power.

### Why is wind energy important?

Wind energy is important because it provides clean and renewable electricity. Many countries around the world have suitable wind conditions. In countries such as Ghana, Denmark or India, strong and steady winds help reduce dependence on fossil fuels and support more sustainable energy systems.

### ✅ Key Advantages of Wind Energy

- **Renewable Energy Source:** Wind is a free natural resource that is constantly replenished, making it a sustainable source of energy.
- **Environmentally Friendly:** Wind energy produces no emissions or pollutants, which helps protect the environment.
- **Low Operating Costs:** After installation, wind turbines require little maintenance, and the wind itself is free.
- **Energy Independence:** Wind energy allows communities to generate their own electricity and reduce reliance on external energy sources.
- **Versatile Applications:** Wind turbines can be used in many settings, from large wind farms to small installations that power homes or businesses.

## 📘 Key Wind Energy Terms

*You can skim or skip this section if you already know these terms.*

- 🎯 **Angle of Attack:** The angle between the blade and the wind. Adjusting this angle can optimize how much energy is captured.
- 📈 **Average Power Density:** Measures how much energy is available per square meter of wind. Higher W/m² means more potential energy for generating electricity.
- 💨 **Bernoulli's Principle:** Explains how faster-moving air creates lower pressure. This principle helps generate lift on turbine blades, making them spin.
- 🚫 **Drag:** The resistance caused by air pushing against the blades. Reducing drag improves efficiency.
- ⚡ **Energy:** Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It exists in many forms, such as heat, light, motion or electricity.
- ⚙️ **Gearbox:** A mechanical system that increases the speed of the turbine shaft to match the requirements of the generator.
- 🔌 **Generator:** The part of the turbine that converts mechanical energy from the spinning blades into electrical energy.
- 📍 **MPP (Maximum Power Point):** The operating condition where a turbine produces the maximum possible power for a given wind speed and blade angle.
- 🌊 **Offshore Wind Turbine:** A wind turbine located at sea. It uses strong ocean winds to produce electricity, which is sent to the onshore power grid.
- 🌄 **Onshore Wind Turbine:** A wind turbine located on land, often in open plains or mountain areas. It turns wind into electricity and feeds it into the power grid.
- 🔋 **Power:** Power is the amount of energy that something can produce or use in a certain amount of time. The unit is Watts (W).
- 🌬️ **Speed of Wind:** How fast the air is moving. Faster wind speeds mean more energy is available for generating power.
- 🔌 **Voltage:** Voltage is like the "pressure" in an electrical circuit. It tells us how strongly the electricity is being pushed through wires. The unit is Volt (V).
- 🌪️ **Wind:** Wind is moving air in the atmosphere. It happens when the air heats up differently in different places and moves around.
- 🔄 **Wind Energy:** Wind energy is the energy created by moving air. Wind turbines turn this energy into electricity.
- 📏 **Wind Speed:** Measures how fast the wind is blowing in meters per second. Higher wind speed means more energy can be harvested by wind turbines.
- 📐 **Work:** In physics, work happens when a force moves an object over a distance.

### 🔌 How to Use a Multimeter

A multimeter is a handy tool that can measure electrical quantities like **voltage**, **current**, and **resistance**.

**Important Steps:**

1. **Turn on the multimeter:** Make sure the multimeter is on by turning the dial.
2. **Select the Measurement Type:**
   - *Voltage (V):* Turn the dial to "V" with a straight line (DC) or wavy line (AC).
   - *Current (A):* Turn the dial to "A". Choose between "mA" and "A" — we usually use mA!
   - *Resistance (Ω):* Turn the dial to the "Ω" symbol.
3. **Set the Range:** Start with the highest setting to avoid damage.
4. **Connect the Probes:** Plug black probe into COM port. Plug red probe into the "VΩmA" port for voltage/resistance, or "10A" for high currents.
5. **Take the Measurement:** Hold the metal tips of the probes against the two points to measure.
6. **Read the Result:** Look at the screen and note the value and unit (V, A, mA, Ω).
7. **Turn Off:** Rotate the dial to OFF.

> ⚠️ **Safety First!** Never measure voltage when set to current or resistance! Start with the highest range setting for safety. Avoid touching probe tips directly.

- Wind energy is a renewable energy source
- Wind energy can be transformed to electricity, but only when there is enough wind
- Wind is created when air heats up unevenly — land warms up faster than water

### 2.1 Making a Paper Wind Rose

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Printed Wind Rose Model, Scissors, Paper cup, Tape, Long thin wooden stick, Two paper clips

**Steps:**
1. Cut out the square shown on page 24 of your workbook.
2. Cut diagonally along the dotted lines until you reach the circle — do not cut the circle.
3. Place the corners one by one in the center so the points lie on top of each other.
4. Fix the aligned corners in the centre with tape.
5. Use scissors to cut a small hole through the tape in the center and insert a wooden stick.
6. Turn the paper cup upside down and attach two paper clips on opposite sides.
7. Insert the stick through the paper clips.

### 2.2 Measuring Wind Speed

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Paper wind rose, Colorful marker, Stopwatch, Fan

**Steps:**
1. Take your wind rose and color each fan with a different colored marker.
2. Place your colored wind rose on a flat, stable surface where it can spin freely.
3. Place a fan about 1.5 m away.
4. Set the fan to level 1 and count how many full rotations the wind rose completes in 1 minute (using a stopwatch).
5. Repeat at different fan speeds and record results in the observation table.

📝 **Exercises:**
- **Task 1:** Fill in the table with your measurements
- **Task 2:** How does wind speed relate to the number of rotations?
- **Task 3:** At which speed did the wind rose rotate the fastest? Explain why.

✅ **Good to know:** Anemometers measure wind speed by counting rotations. Open areas or high altitudes are best for wind energy projects.

### 2.3 Wind Can Do Work

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Paper wind rose, 30cm light thread, Tape, Fan, Stopwatch, Paper clip

**Steps:**
1. Tie one end of the thread to the end of the wooden stick. Make a loop at the free end and attach a paper clip as a weight.
2. Place the paper wind rose on a flat surface where it can rotate freely.
3. Place a fan about 1.5 m away.
4. Set the fan to low speed and measure how long it takes for the paper clip to lift.
5. Repeat at different fan speeds and record results.

📝 **Exercises:**
- **Task 1:** Fill in the observation table
- **Task 2:** How does wind speed affect the speed at which the wind rose lifts the weights?
- **Task 3:** How does the weight of the load affect the wind rose's ability to lift it at different wind speeds?
- **Task 4:** How can this experiment relate to real wind turbines?

> **Reflective question:** Wind power is not always available at the same level. Can a modern society really rely on such a fluctuating energy source? How should we deal with these fluctuations?

✅ **Good to know:** Wind can perform mechanical work. Real wind turbines also rely on wind conditions and design choices to work efficiently.

### 3.1 Let's Make an Airfoil!

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Narrow wooden stick, Adhesive tape, 1 sheet of paper DIN A4 (or 2× DIN A5), Hairdryer

**Steps:**
1. Cut the A4 page in the middle of the long side.
2. Place the two sheets on top of each other so they overlap properly.
3. Tape the two sheets together on one short side so they open like a book.
4. Lift the top sheet and tape it slightly further toward the center of the bottom sheet (about 3 finger-widths). The top sheet should be curved; the bottom flat.
5. Push the wooden stick through the opening along the side with the larger curve.
6. Hold the ends of the wooden stick with both hands and blow across the top surface or use a hairdryer.

📝 **Exercises:**
- **Task 1:** What happens if you blow over the top surface?
- **Task 2:** What could be the reason for that observation?
- **Task 3:** Based on your observations, what shape should the blades have?
- **Task 4:** What factors could influence the output power of wind turbines?

### 3.2 Making Flow Visible

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Paper wing (from previous experiment), Adhesive tape, Thread or thin yarn (~5cm), Hairdryer

**Steps:**
1. Glue small wool threads onto the upper (curved) side of the paper wing (2–3 in a row in the center).
2. Hold the wing in the airflow of the hairdryer (directed at the center, distance ~10cm).
3. Move the wing sideways while the hairdryer remains in the same position.
4. Write down your observations.

📝 **Exercises:**
- **Task 1:** What do you observe about the threads when you blow over the surface?
- **Task 2:** The wing only lifts when the threads lie smoothly against the wing. Why?

> **Reflective Question:** Should engineers working on safety-critical technology rely mainly on proven designs, or should they test new, potentially riskier designs? Who should take responsibility if an innovation fails?

✅ **Good to know about airfoils:**
- Airfoils have a curved upper surface and a flatter lower surface
- When air flows over them, the pressure on the upper surface is lower — this pressure difference generates **lift**
- **Laminar flow:** When the wing is correctly oriented, the threads lie smoothly and the wing lifts
- **Flow separation:** When the wing moves sideways, threads flutter and the wing sinks — the air can no longer follow the curvature

### 4.1 Understanding Power Measurement

To measure the power generated by the wind turbine, the experiments use a **multimeter**. We use **voltage** to estimate power.

- **What is power?** Power is the amount of energy something produces per unit of time (Watts, W).
- **What is voltage?** Voltage is like the "pressure" in an electrical circuit. Higher voltage means more electrical energy is moving.
- **Why use voltage?** When the wind turbine produces more power, the voltage often increases — it directly reflects how much electrical energy is produced.

### 4.2 How to Use the Wind Turbine Kit

🧪 **Experiment**

**Materials needed:** Wind set with 3 blades (medium size), Fan, Multimeter

**Steps:**
1. Place your wind kit in front of the fan. Attach three blades (medium size) to your wind kit.
2. Attach the nacelle with a screw. The angle of the blades should be flat.
3. Measure the voltage with the multimeter at low, medium, and high fan speeds for the following orientations:
   - Front view (facing directly into the wind)
   - Turned to the side (45°)
   - Turned to the side (90°)
